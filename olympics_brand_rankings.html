Analysis & Insights

General Insights

Universal Coca-Cola Dominance: Coca-Cola achieved unanimous #1 ranking across all LLMs for "brands that come to mind," demonstrating exceptional mental availability despite not dominating equipment or athlete performance categories. This reflects decades of consistent Olympic storytelling and emotional campaigns.

The Official vs. Reality Gap: Every LLM recognized the critical distinction between official Olympic sponsors (Coca-Cola, Visa, Samsung, Toyota) and the technical brands athletes actually rely on (Atomic, Salomon, Rossignol, Burton). This gap represents both opportunity and riskofficial sponsors gain visibility, but equipment brands own credibility.

Local vs. Global Sponsor Hierarchy: Milano Cortina 2026 features a unique dual-layer structure where IOC Worldwide TOP Partners (10 brands with category exclusivity) coexist with Italian domestic partners (Eni, Poste Italiane, TIM, Intesa Sanpaolo). DeepSeek was most attuned to this local dimension.

Fashion Meets Function: The Olympics increasingly blend performance and lifestyle, with fashion brands (Ralph Lauren, Skims, Lululemon, EA7 Emporio Armani) competing for mental share alongside technical equipment brands. Gemini particularly emphasized this cultural crossover.

Insights by LLM

ChatGPT: Provided the most balanced, consensus-driven rankings heavily weighted toward IOC TOP Partners. Showed strong adherence to official hierarchies with minimal deviation across questions. Most "corporate-safe" responses reflecting established Olympic marketing doctrine.

Gemini: Demonstrated unique fashion and lifestyle awareness, uniquely ranking EA7 Emporio Armani, Prada, and Ralph Lauren highly in association questions. Drew from ISPO trade fair intelligence and Business of Fashion sources, showing cultural trend sensitivity beyond pure sponsorship contracts. Most attuned to the "lifestyle halo" effect.

Claude: Took a notably cautious approach, acknowledging knowledge limitations and recommending verification searches. Only LLM to explicitly flag uncertainty about current sponsor status. Most transparent about epistemological boundaries, though this limited actionable specificity in rankings.

Perplexity: Provided the most comprehensive source transparency, including Skims in top 10 association despite non-TOP statusreflecting real-world buzz around Team USA partnerships. Drew from widest source diversity including hospitality sites and consumer coverage. Most "reality-checked" against actual market conversation.

Grok: Included surprising entries like Budweiser and Gatorade in mental availability rankings, potentially reflecting U.S. market bias. Showed awareness of brands like Red Bull that punch above their official sponsor weight through athlete individual sponsorships. Most willing to diverge from official sponsor lists.

DeepSeek: Most methodologically rigorous, emphasizing verified data from SportBusiness databases and distinguishing clearly between Worldwide, Premium, and Official Partner tiers. Uniquely foregrounded Italian domestic sponsors (Eni, Poste Italiane, TIM, Intesa Sanpaolo) reflecting genuine Milano Cortina 2026 reality. Most "local market fluent."

Non-Olympic Sponsors That Appear + Why

Red Bull: Appeared in 5 of 6 LLM responses despite zero official Olympic sponsorship. Reason: Dominant individual athlete sponsorships across snowboarding, skiing, and freestyle create powerful "winter sports" mental association. Demonstrates how athlete-first strategy can rival official partnerships in mental availability.

Ralph Lauren: Ranked by all LLMs despite being a Team USA partner, not IOC TOP sponsor. Reason: Opening ceremony uniforms create outsized visibility and cultural moments. Single high-profile touchpoint (ceremony broadcast) rivals year-round sponsor activation in memorability.

Skims: Uniquely flagged by Perplexity and Gemini. Reason: High-buzz Team USA 2026 base layer partnership with athlete-modeled campaigns generated disproportionate social media conversation. Shows how strategic NOC partnerships by fashion-forward brands can create "unofficial sponsor" perception.

Nike: Consistently ranked despite not being IOC TOP Partner. Reason: Combination of Team USA competition wear partnerships, decades of Olympic athlete endorsements, and general "athletic excellence" brand equity. Mental availability built over 40+ years of Olympic athlete storytelling transcends current contract status.

NBC/Peacock: Appeared in "mental availability" rankings. Reason: Broadcasting association in key markets (especially U.S.) creates de facto sponsorship perception. Viewers conflate broadcast rights with sponsorship, demonstrating media platform power in shaping Olympic brand landscape.

McDonald's: Noted by multiple LLMs as "historical" association despite ending TOP sponsorship after Rio 2016. Reason: Four-decade sponsorship (1976-2017) created enduring mental availability that persists 8+ years after contract end. Demonstrates extremely long half-life of Olympic brand equity.

Burton, Atomic, Salomon, Rossignol: Technical equipment brands consistently ranked despite no official IOC sponsorship. Reason: On-snow visibility, athlete reliance, and federation partnerships create "earned" Olympic association. Every close-up of skis/boards at finish line is unpaid brand exposure worth millions.

Key Strategic Implications

For Official Sponsors: Massive gap between "official status" and "mental availability" or "athlete reality." TOP partnership guarantees category exclusivity but not consumer relevance. Activation quality matters more than contract value.

For Non-Sponsors: Multiple pathways to Olympic association without TOP contracts: (1) Individual athlete sponsorships, (2) Team/NOC partnerships, (3) Equipment provision, (4) Historical equity, (5) Adjacent category dominance. Red Bull and Nike prove unofficial pathways can rival official sponsorship in mental availability.

For Brands Considering Olympic Investment: LLM responses reflect the fragmented reality of Olympic sponsorship hierarchies. Success requires clarity on objective: seeking official legitimacy, building athlete credibility, generating cultural conversation, or creating long-term mental availability. Different objectives demand different strategies.

Strategic Questions for Sponsors

This scorecard reveals critical gaps between sponsorship investment and AI visibility. Every brand needs to answer these questions:

1. Are sponsorship benefits weighted to "answer visibility"?

The Gap: Traditional packages emphasize signage and broadcast logos but ignore AI discoverability. Red Bull appears across LLMs despite zero sponsorship, while some TOP partners are invisible. Action: Demand AI visibility metrics in contracts and "preferred mention" rights in official content.

2. Is your brand optimizing owned assets for AI discovery?

The Gap: AI synthesizes from brand websites, press releases, and athlete partnershipsnot just Olympic.org. Skims gained visibility through owned content; Italian sponsors won via clear web presence. Action: Create dedicated Olympic pages, publish athlete stories, use structured data, update Wikipedia entries.

3. Are TV ads and activations transcribed for AI readability?

The Gap: AI can't watch commercials or visit fan zones. Coca-Cola's #1 ranking reflects decades of documented campaigns. Without text-based documentation, million-dollar activations are invisible. Action: Publish activation summaries, create blog posts with metadata, submit marketing case studies.

4. How vulnerable is your brand to negative AI associations?

The Gap: LLMs surface controversies and sustainability critiques alongside sponsorships. A single negative article can dominate AI responses. Action: Monitor AI responses for negative associations, publish transparent sustainability data, use W.R.A.P. framework for narrative control.

5. Are you measuring what matters in the Answer Economy?

The Gap: Six AI systems produced six different hierarchies. Traditional ROI metrics (impressions, social engagement) miss AI-mediated brand discovery.
