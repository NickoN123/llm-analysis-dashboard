Executive Summary

In 1998, Procter & Gamble convened the advertising industry's most consequential gathering—the FAST Summit—to confront the internet's disruption of consumer trust. Nearly three decades later, the industry stands at a second, more profound inflection point. Artificial intelligence is not a new channel. It is a new intermediary of trust—reshaping how consumers form beliefs, make decisions, and extend authority to brands. The challenges of the AI era are not merely technical; they are epistemological. Advertising's claim on consumer trust must be earned all over again—this time before the architecture of decision-making becomes opaque and uncontrollable. Collective, standards-based leadership is once again required.

I
Part One
FAST: When the Industry Confronted Itself

A Market Expanding Faster Than Its Foundations

In August 1998, Procter & Gamble—under the leadership of Global Chief Marketing Officer Denis Beausejour—convened one of the most consequential gatherings in modern marketing history: the Future of Advertising Stakeholders (FAST) Summit. This was not a conference in the conventional sense. It was a deliberate act of industry leadership—a recognition that the rise of the internet had exposed structural weaknesses in advertising that no single company could solve alone. At its core was a deceptively simple but profoundly urgent question: could advertising earn trust in a medium where consumers—not marketers—controlled engagement?

By 1998, internet advertising was growing rapidly—but on unstable ground. Marketers were experimenting, publishers were monetizing, and new formats were emerging, yet the system lacked coherence. Measurement was inconsistent, buying processes were fragmented, and creative formats were constrained by early technical limitations. More critically, consumer acceptance was deeply uncertain. Users were beginning to resist intrusive formats, and early signs of "banner blindness" were already appearing. Denis Beausejour recognized that the issue was not one of execution—it was structural. The industry had, in his words, "built a box" around digital advertising, relying on familiar constructs that failed to reflect how consumers actually behaved online. Despite P&G's scale and influence, even it could not justify meaningful reallocation of brand budgets without greater confidence in the medium's integrity.

The Cincinnati Summit: A System-Level Intervention

The FAST Summit, held August 20–21, 1998, at P&G's headquarters in Cincinnati, brought together a cross-section of the entire advertising ecosystem in a manner that was extraordinary then—and remains rare today. Global advertisers including P&G, Coca-Cola, AT&T, McDonald's, Unilever, and Colgate-Palmolive sat alongside agencies, publishers, early internet platforms, technology providers, and major industry bodies including the ANA, the IAB, and the ARF. This was not a working group focused on incremental improvement. It was a recognition that the entire system required alignment.

Rich LeFurgy, then president of the IAB, underscored the moment's significance when he noted that participants would need to "check our individual agendas at the door." Beausejour himself described the gathering as potentially "a seminal moment in the history of online advertising." Ad Age coverage at the time reinforced that sense of urgency—portraying the summit as a pivotal acknowledgment that the industry could not simply optimize its way forward; it had to come together to fix the fundamentals before digital advertising could reach its full potential.

The Breakthrough Realization: Power Had Shifted Permanently

What FAST crystallized—perhaps more than any prior industry effort—was that the internet had fundamentally altered the balance of power between brands and consumers. As AAAA executive John Kamp stated plainly: "On the Internet, the consumer has control." This was observable in behavior: consumers could ignore advertising entirely, switch brands with minimal friction, amplify opinions publicly, and choose whether to engage at all. For an industry built on controlled exposure and predictable reach, this represented a profound structural disruption. FAST forced the industry to confront a reality it had not fully internalized: advertising was no longer something done to consumers—it was something negotiated with them.

The Four Pillars of FAST

The summit organized its work around four interconnected priorities, each addressing a distinct structural failure in the emerging digital advertising system:

01
Consumer Acceptance

FAST placed consumer acceptance at the center of its agenda, recognizing that digital advertising would not scale unless it was perceived as valuable, relevant, and respectful. This was an early articulation of what would become core principles of modern marketing: permission, personalization, and utility. Crucially, FAST reframed the question—from "how do we deliver ads?" to "why would consumers accept them at all?"

02
Measurement & Standards

Measurement was widely viewed as the industry's most urgent operational challenge. Without shared definitions, advertisers could not trust reported outcomes. The ARF-FAST Principles focused on establishing a common language—defining impressions as "opportunity to see," addressing technical distortions such as caching, and introducing the now-familiar triad of site-centric, ad-centric, and user-centric measurement.

03
Ad Models & Formats

Beausejour's critique of the industry's "box" was most visible in the discussion of ad formats. The banner ad, while ubiquitous, was insufficient for brand-building. FAST encouraged exploration of interactive units, sequential storytelling, and richer immersive experiences—grounded in the conviction that the medium should shape the message, not the reverse.

04
Media Buying Efficiency

FAST addressed the operational complexity of digital media buying. Fragmented systems, inconsistent pricing, and lack of standardization made it difficult for large advertisers to scale investment. By simplifying and standardizing these processes, FAST helped lay the groundwork for more structured digital marketplaces—enabling the eventual flow of substantial brand budgets into the medium.

FAST Forward: Institutionalizing Change

FAST did not end with the summit. It evolved into FAST Forward, a coordinated initiative led by ANA, AAAA, IAB, and ARF. A 15-member steering committee was formed, and working groups were established across the four priority areas. By 2000, the ARF-FAST Principles had formalized key aspects of digital measurement and accountability, directly influencing subsequent standards developed by the IAB and Media Rating Council. What FAST ultimately accomplished was not just progress on specific issues—it created a shared framework for how the industry would evolve collectively.

Part One and a Half
The Unfinished Work of FAST:
How Scale Outpaced Trust

What makes FAST so relevant today is not just what it set in motion—but what it did not fully resolve. While FAST established a shared agenda around consumer acceptance, standards, and trust, the industry's subsequent evolution took a different path: one driven less by those principles and more by the powerful economics of targeting, automation, and platform scale. In the early 2000s, and accelerating through the 2010s, digital advertising did achieve what FAST aimed for in one critical dimension: scale. Measurement became more standardized. Buying became more efficient. Budgets flowed. But that growth came with significant trade-offs.

The Shift: From Consumer Acceptance to Targeting Precision

Where FAST emphasized consumer acceptance, the industry increasingly optimized for targeting precision and performance efficiency. The rise of search, social platforms, and programmatic advertising created a system where success was defined less by whether consumers welcomed advertising, and more by whether it could be delivered to the right person at the right moment with measurable outcomes. This shift was not accidental—it was economically rational. Platforms that could aggregate user data at scale, enable precise targeting, and deliver measurable ROI quickly became dominant. Yet this model subtly reframed the role of the consumer: from an active participant whose acceptance mattered, to a data source whose behavior could be predicted and influenced.

The Platform Era: Power Re-Concentrated

FAST began with the premise that power had shifted to consumers. Yet over time, power re-concentrated—this time in the hands of a small number of platform intermediaries that controlled data, distribution, measurement, and optimization algorithms. These platforms became the gatekeepers of digital advertising, representing the majority of global media spend. Consumer visibility into how and why they were being targeted diminished substantially. The result was a system that scaled rapidly, but one in which the principles of transparency and user understanding were routinely subordinated to the imperatives of performance and efficiency.

The Trust Gap Widens

By the late 2010s and into the 2020s, many of the very issues FAST sought to address began to resurface—this time at scale. Opaque targeting practices, the blurring of content and advertising in native and social formats, the proliferation of low-quality or misleading content amplified by performance incentives, and the erosion of consumer trust in digital environments all reflected a system where self-governance had lagged badly behind innovation. Regulatory responses—from GDPR to CCPA and beyond—can be seen, in part, as the external correction of that failure.

In hindsight, the industry did not ignore trust—it deferred it. FAST asked whether advertising could adapt to a consumer-controlled environment. The industry's answer, over the following two decades, was to build systems that could work around consumer control—through targeting, automation, and optimization—rather than fully aligning with it. Digital advertising became extraordinarily effective. And simultaneously, it became increasingly scrutinized and mistrusted. The foundational tension FAST identified—between advertising ambition and consumer autonomy—was never fully resolved. It was scaled.

II
Part Two
The AI Era: Trust Moves Upstream

From Medium to Mediator of Decisions

If the internet changed how advertising is delivered, AI changes how decisions are formed. AI systems do not simply present information; they interpret it, synthesize it, and increasingly act on it. They operate not as passive channels, but as active intermediaries of trust. Research conducted by BrandRank.AI in partnership with the ANA highlights a critical shift: AI advertising is fundamentally different because it is delivered within systems that speak as trusted advisors rather than media platforms. This creates a dynamic in which commercial influence is no longer adjacent to content—it is embedded within the reasoning process itself.

The Speed of Adoption Changes Everything

What makes this shift categorically different from prior media disruptions is the sheer velocity at which it is happening. Leading AI platforms are already processing over one billion prompts per day—a pace of consumer adoption that dwarfs the early years of search, social, or mobile. Consumers are not gradually migrating to AI-assisted decision-making; they are arriving en masse, bringing with them an enormous range of needs, intentions, and trust assumptions that the industry has barely begun to map. At this scale, the window for establishing norms before behaviors calcify is not years—it is months.

This speed also reframes the strategic imperative for brands. For two decades, the dominant optimization challenge was conversational: how to reach consumers at the right moment in a defined media context, with the right message, at the right price. AI changes the fundamental unit of consumer engagement from the impression to the question. Consumers are not browsing or scrolling—they are inquiring, exploring, and reasoning out loud. The brand that understands how to earn visibility inside that process is not optimizing for conversation. It is optimizing for curiosity—meeting consumers at the precise moment they are forming intent, not after it has already been shaped by someone else.

What Makes This Moment More Complex—and More Fragile

The current moment introduces conditions that go beyond anything the industry faced in 1998. The challenges are not just technical or operational; they are epistemological—they affect how consumers determine what is true, credible, and worthy of action. Unlike the early web, where the risk was that advertising would simply be ignored, the risk in AI is that it may be trusted too readily, without sufficient transparency about the commercial forces shaping what the system says.

Dimension FAST Era (1998) AI Era (2026)
Core challenge Credibility of a new digital medium Integrity of AI decision systems
Consumer stance Skeptical of advertising Trusting of AI outputs
Primary risk Ineffective or ignored advertising Misguided, autonomous decisions
Solution focus Standards and ad formats Trust architecture and governance
Power dynamic Consumers gaining control AI systems mediating control

Trust Transfer and the Illusion of Neutrality

AI systems benefit from what can be described as trust transfer: the credibility of the underlying system extends to the recommendations it generates. Consumers do not approach AI outputs with the same skepticism they apply to traditional advertising. Instead, they interpret them as objective, synthesized judgments. When commercial influence is introduced into that context—especially if it is not clearly disclosed—it risks being perceived not as persuasion, but as truth. BrandRank.AI research shows strong consensus across AI systems that undisclosed commercial influence in such contexts is categorically unacceptable, particularly in sensitive domains such as health, finance, and emotional vulnerability.

From Persuasion to Action: The Agentic Shift

Perhaps the most significant evolution is the rise of agentic AI—systems that do not merely recommend, but act. Booking travel, selecting products, managing finances: these are no longer hypothetical use cases. This shift fundamentally changes the nature of advertising risk. In traditional contexts, misleading advertising might influence perception. In agentic systems, it can result in decisions being executed before the consumer is even aware of the influence. Research reveals unanimous agreement across AI engines: undisclosed commercial influence in these contexts is not merely problematic—it is unacceptable. This represents a new standard for the industry, one that demands pre-action transparency and explicit user control.

The Questions the Industry Must Now Answer

The AI era presents a new set of foundational questions that mirror—and materially extend—the challenges addressed by FAST. These are not theoretical. They are already shaping how AI systems are designed, deployed, and monetized. Each reflects a distinct dimension of the trust challenge that collective industry leadership must address.

1
What constitutes fair influence within an AI-generated answer?

Traditional advertising operates within understood conventions—consumers know they are seeing a paid message. Inside AI-generated answers, the boundaries are opaque. When a brand is surfaced prominently in response to a consumer query, is that organic relevance or commercial positioning? Without a shared definition distinguishing earned visibility from paid placement, the concept of fair competition in the AI era is meaningless.

2
Where is the boundary between optimization and manipulation?

Brands and agencies are already working to optimize their presence within AI answer systems—shaping content, structuring data, and influencing the signals that AI engines use to generate responses. At what point does optimization shade into manipulation? The industry needs a shared definition of acceptable optimization practices—and clear red lines for conduct that undermines system integrity.

3
How should brands participate in systems that mediate decisions rather than deliver messages?

The advertising industry was built around message delivery: reach the right audience with the right creative at the right moment. AI systems do not deliver messages—they mediate decisions. Brands must fundamentally rethink their role: from interruptors and persuaders to information providers whose credibility and relevance within AI training data determines their visibility. This requires new competencies, new metrics, and new strategies—none of which currently have agreed-upon standards.

4
What does meaningful disclosure look like when timing determines trust?

In traditional media, disclosure conventions are well-established: "Sponsored," "Paid Advertisement," "#ad." In AI systems, the timing and format of disclosure are far more complex. A disclosure that appears after a recommendation has already been integrated into a consumer's decision-making process may be technically compliant but functionally meaningless. The industry must develop disclosure frameworks that are contextually appropriate, genuinely informative, and consistently applied across AI platforms.

5
Who is accountable when AI systems act autonomously on behalf of consumers?

Agentic AI introduces a new accountability gap. When an AI system books a flight, selects a product, or executes a financial transaction on a consumer's behalf—and that action is shaped by undisclosed commercial influence—who bears responsibility? The brand? The platform? The agency? The developer? Traditional advertising accountability frameworks, built around disclosed paid placements with identifiable sponsors, are not equipped to answer this question. New governance structures are urgently needed.

III
Part Three
Why This Moment Requires
FAST-Level Leadership

The Same Pattern, With Higher Stakes

The parallels to 1998 are unmistakable: rapid growth, fragmented practices, and a lack of shared standards in a medium that is reshaping how consumers relate to commercial influence. But the implications are more profound. The industry is no longer simply building a new advertising channel; it is shaping the infrastructure of decision-making itself. What is at risk is not merely advertising effectiveness—it is the integrity of consumer trust in AI systems more broadly. And unlike the early web, where the failure mode was ineffective advertising, the failure mode in AI is something worse: advertising that is effective precisely because it is invisible.

The Case for Collective Action

The FAST Summit demonstrated that systemic challenges require systemic solutions. No single company—no matter how large—can define the rules of an ecosystem alone. Today, the need for coordination is even more urgent:

  • Without shared standards, inconsistent practices will erode trust across the entire AI ecosystem—damaging not only the brands that behave badly, but those that do not
  • Without proactive leadership, regulation will fill the gap—potentially in ways that constrain legitimate innovation or impose frameworks poorly suited to how AI systems actually function
  • Without alignment, brands risk losing visibility and influence within AI systems that increasingly mediate consumer choices—not because they failed to advertise, but because they failed to build credibility in the systems that matter

Completing the Work of FAST: The Role of ANA

The ANA is uniquely positioned to lead this next phase—not by replicating FAST, but by extending its principles into a new domain. The organization's credibility, convening authority, and cross-industry relationships make it the natural home for the kind of collective effort that the moment demands. This work requires five interconnected commitments:

Priority 01
Convene the Full Ecosystem

Bring together AI platforms, brands, agencies, standards bodies, and consumer advocates to establish shared norms—just as FAST convened a previously fragmented digital advertising industry around common principles.

Priority 02
Define Principles for Transparency

Establish clear, actionable principles for disclosure, transparency, and accountability in AI-mediated systems—beginning with the five foundational questions in Part II and building toward enforceable standards.

Priority 03
Build New Measurement Frameworks

Develop metrics that capture influence and outcomes within AI systems—not just exposure. "Answer share," visibility ranking, and sentiment within AI-generated responses must become as measurable and standardized as impressions were after FAST.

Priority 04
Create High-Risk Guardrails

Establish governance guardrails for contexts where consumer trust is most vulnerable—health, finance, children's products, and agentic AI—where the consequences of undisclosed commercial influence are most severe.

Priority 05
Champion Trust as Foundation

Reaffirm—loudly and consistently—that trust is not a constraint on advertising but its foundation. The industry that loses sight of this in the AI era will face the same crisis of legitimacy that FAST was convened to prevent, only at far greater scale.

Conclusion
Back to the Future

FAST asked whether advertising could adapt to a world where consumers were in control. Today's question is more complex and more consequential: can advertising adapt to a world where AI systems control how information is interpreted, decisions are made, and actions are taken—often before consumers are fully aware that commercial forces have shaped what they see and hear?

The lesson from 1998 is not simply that the industry must adapt—it is that adaptation requires intentional, collective effort. Trust does not emerge automatically. It must be designed into systems from the beginning, embedded in standards, and maintained through accountability structures that evolve alongside the technology. The companies and institutions that understand this—and act on it—will help define the AI era on terms that are sustainable for the industry and genuinely valuable for consumers.

We are, in many ways, back where we started: facing a new medium, new behaviors, and new uncertainties about the role of commercial influence in an environment consumers are only beginning to understand. But this time, the challenge is not just to make advertising work. It is to ensure that it works without compromising the very trust on which the future of the entire ecosystem depends—a trust that, once lost in AI systems, may be far more difficult to reclaim than it was in the early days of the web.
